Strike

When I was young, I used to sit out with my parents and watch the storms roll by. We’d gather round the porch as startling purple darted down to the ground in forking spears on humid nights. We’d listen to the thunder, sipping on milkshakes and juice boxes as the rolling pastures were lit up by the light. It would wake me whenever my eyes grew heavy, until my tiredness grew too strong and I’d fall asleep, bundled up against the side of one of my parents.

One night, we were all crowded around our squeaky radio, Dad shifting the antenna as the newsman’s voice blurred in and out. The pieces that managed to make it through the crackle of the static were strange words I didn’t understand. Mum’s eyebrows creased into a sharp frown as she turned to me, kneeling down and trying to put up a smile as she pulled me into a hug. The storms were getting too close to watch tonight, she told me.

I didn’t understand. How could something this amazing be too close? I didn’t ask her though. I knew better than to bother mum when she seemed stressed.

And when it got a little later, without the lightning to watch, my parents decided we should all try to get an early night. But I couldn’t sleep. I could hear it out there, booming down through the sweltering heat. Every time my curtains lit up, I would close my eyes tightly, the smile irrepressible on my face as I waited for that wall of sound to run through me.

It got to a point where I couldn’t take it. I had to go and see it for myself. I threw off the covers of my bedspread, climbed off the mattress and gathered up my rain gear. I was swallowed up in my ruffled raincoat, my nose peeking out above the collar and my bright red rain boots pushing out under the hem. Despite the clomping of the shoes, I did my best to creep across the echoey old floorboards. I wished very quietly that the claps of thunder would cover my barely sneaky journey out the back door and onto the porch.

I was immediately struck by the beauty. Far up above in the darkness of the night, jets of sparking light helped my vision adjust. Grey pockets of cloud were connected by arcing streams of electricity. A constant rumble echoed through the sky, a brilliant background noise that made me bounce in my rainboots in excitement.

I was too giddy, I felt dizzy with the energy that coursed through me, like I was floating. I clapped my palms together as I positively tingled at the sight.

All at once, I felt the sensation spike as a bright shaft of jagged light crashed down to the ground in a moment. I could barely gauge how close it was to me before the thunder rang out with an almighty bang, an eruption of noise that felt like it started in my bones. I threw my hands over my ears in a rush, an involuntary shout pulled out of my stomach and swallowed up by the cacophony that roared through the whole world.

Even a minute later, I was still struck by the shock of it, hands still slapped against the sides of my head as my teeth rattled and echoes continued to reverberate through my very being.

I hadn’t ever felt like that before. Shocked, motionless, as my brain tried to reconnect with my body. Dad picked me up at some point and rushed back into the house, where mum pried my hands down so she could look at my ears.

They scolded me once I came back to reality, and I cried as they bundled me up in a hug. But even through all of that, even as I shook and listened to my parents, the sound of thunder tearing through the sky rattled through my mind. The booming noise that wouldn’t leave me.

15 years later, I still feel it sometimes. A bit of static in my fingers, my teeth shaking in my mouth. That explosion going off right next to me. Whenever my adrenaline spikes, it rings through me like a gong, the crash announcing the panic about to grab hold of me.
