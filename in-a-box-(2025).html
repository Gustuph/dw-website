In a Box

We used to go to my grandad’s farm every Easter. He had a falling out with my mum when I was young, so I only have fleeting memories of him, and only in pieces. I remember the constant smell of cigarettes, the tired eyes, the big, wide grin, knowing he’d just made a joke that my mother wouldn’t approve of telling us. I grew up deep in suburbia, twists and turns off of main roads. I wasn’t reared for farm life. But anyone who saw him knew he was right at home rounding up his cows and sheep.


There’s a famous philosophical thought exercise referred to as the Ship of Theseus that I quite like. The setup goes that Theseus sails his ship on adventure after adventure. Every time he comes back to port, he replaces a single board of the ship. Once all the individual pieces of the ship have been replaced, is it still the same Ship of Theseus? I love telling people little facts and ideas like this. My father would say they were tidbits. My sister would say that it’s up to the other person in the conversation to decide if your facts are fun.


The first time I thought there might have been something wrong with me was around year 9 of high school. Everybody was finding partners, even if they only lasted a week. Then there was me. One day, a friend came to me beaming. Not a virgin anymore, he said through a voice crack. I wasn’t sure how to respond. I couldn’t figure out how to tell him that I didn’t care. Every variation made me sound like a jealous, defensive dork. I was pretty sure that’s all I was back then. That or just plain broken, because I wasn’t anything like my classmates.


When my nephew could only really communicate in terms of crying and not crying, he couldn’t stand being warm. He got swaddled and immediately he’d be struggling and squirming until he got his arms out. I relate to him strongly on that. I take after my father when it comes to the weather, we both sweat a flood at any time of any day. It’s the Scotsman in us, he told me once. The idiot that thought he belonged in this heat. Should’ve known then and there to head back for the north.


In my first round of university, I was reading a psychology textbook, trawling through dense language about the parts of the brain until I got to the amygdala. A tiny piece of the brain, the size of a shelled peanut, described as responsible for the 4 F’s of human motivation – fight, flight, feed and mate. The 4 F’s came up again in a tutorial, when we were asked what makes a person. A confident guy I envied put up his hand from a central table and loudly announced we were made human by our motivations. He particularly highlighted the desire for romantic companionship. I didn’t correct him. I just felt inhuman.


I’d been to one funeral before Granddad’s. I was too young to really know what was going on in that one. I don’t even remember my relation to the person who died. I certainly didn’t know what it really meant for her to die. But I remember it was held in a church. I remember the tall arching roofs, the stained glass, the crosses and statues of people that were probably important. I’m not Christian, I wasn’t baptised, wasn’t taught scripture. But I knew even then that nothing in that building was mine to claim, and I didn’t want to try.


A university writing course is such an interesting place to be. The people you meet all have their own inspirations, guided by goals to emulate and be named alongside them. Poets and playwrights and honest to goodness Authors. And the workshops will show you just how close each one is, how talented and strong they are in their craft. I wake up every day with a YA fantasy on my bedside. I take my backpack into the city with a loose sci-fi sprawl in the front pocket and all I can wonder is if I wasn’t accepted by mistake.


Grandad’s death was a shock that came at an inopportune time for my family. He was the second of my grandparents to die in a month, one from each of my parents’ sides of the family. He was a long-term smoker, and according to my mother’s beliefs, had undiagnosed coeliac’s disease. It wasn’t a complication from either of those that killed him, though. He didn’t let either one get that far. I sometimes worry if that’s the part of him I inherited the most of.


I never had much chance of avoiding my growth into a nerd. With my father’s first screening of The Lord of the Rings for me, my fate was sealed. Yet for some reason, most of my friends through school were sporty. Footballers, basketballers, a stray parkour enthusiast. I was so much more interested in being cool than being around people I related to. I can’t say I’m still having that issue though. Not when I run a dungeons and dragons campaign for some family and friends whenever I get the chance.


My parents kept a box of me when I was a kid. Kindergarten pictures, achievement cards, school reports and photos, portfolios, writing. I have it now, up in my room. Every now and then I pull it down, spread the materials out on the floor or the bed, and just bask in it all. It summons up a melancholy that fills time better than anything else I know. I don’t recognise myself in much of it. I lose myself in the funhouse mirrors of my school pictures, the cringe that swallows me when I read my old attempts at story, and most of all, the foreign thoughts that I remember filtering through me back then. The shame. The doubt. Every plank of me has been replaced.
