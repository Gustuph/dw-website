Prognosis

Glenn shouldn’t have driven home. His sweaty form melted into the driver’s seat as the engine finished ticking down to its resting silence. His thoughts floated through his sloshing brain and he pressed the back of his scalp against the cool fabric of the headrest. He soaked in the sensory reprieve as he tried to deepen his shallow breaths and focus on his fingernails biting into his palms until he could finally think straight.

His eyes were set on the badge in the centre of the steering wheel. He couldn’t move them. Couldn’t take the key from its slot. Couldn’t open the car door and stand up. Because that would mean looking toward the entrance to his home. It would mean going inside and having the conversation that awaited him. It would mean time would move forward, drawing him closer to the reality he’d be shoving onto his family, the life he’d burden them with.

Then the migraine reappeared, and he felt his head tilting to the left, jaw coming to touch his shoulder as everything seemed to shake and grow distant and turn in his head, leaving the rest of his body in a strange state of anti-gravity and anti-sensation. As it began to ebb away, he took a deep breath, biting the inside of his cheek to focus on something else, something gentler, that he could make stop at any time.

At least I won’t have to deal with these damned headaches when I die.

He caught the thought before it could linger. Caught it and strangled it.

He jolted his neck back upright, brain fogging as he moved with such speed. It took him a second to find and snatch the key. He focused on the subsiding pain, the dissipating dizziness. He didn’t acknowledge that he had thought the words, tried to forget them and focused on moving. He couldn’t think about the sentiment, he wouldn’t.

Instead, Glenn shoved open the door, legs falling out after it, and stumbled across the tightly-mown yard on unstable feet.

Eventually, he had the door unlocked, and he moved into the dining room, kitchen counter off to the left, where Jess was tidying up the surfaces for him to make his much adored risotto. He didn’t say anything as his wife turned to him at the creak of the front door. Didn’t say anything as she smiled at him. He could see as she took in the lines of his face that she knew what his silence meant. The smile fell from her lips, and she dropped the dish cloth in her hands to rush around the bench and crush herself into his chest. He smothered his face in her hair and sobbed.


Bailey came home not long after. One of the friends Glenn couldn’t remember the name of tagged along to hang out after school. They were rowdy and laughing, jokes flying quickly between them, speaking words only half understood by Glenn. Bailey was too wrapped up in the fun of having a friend over to take special note of his father’s presence in the house, a rarity at this time on a weekday. His son was always so busy. Busy with school or clubs or watching a new movie. Even when he was in the house, he seemed always busy with his friends, ever-connected to them by the phone in his pocket or the computer in his room that he’d assembled over the summer.

It was good that he was busy though. Good that he had friends he could talk to, friends he could count on. A strong support network behind him.

Once the fun had abated, and his friend had gone home, they sat down for dinner, where he listened to Jess and Bailey talk about their day. He relished the conversation. It was a joy in the mundane he hadn’t actively appreciated for a while. It was also a procrastination he was thankful for.

“How was your day, Dad?” Bailey asked the question all too soon.

“Well, I had the doctor today.” He placed the cutlery down so as not to cause a clatter. He cleared his throat, took a breath and looked back up to his son across the table, seeing his expectation. Glenn kept his words slow and gentle, and they were accompanied by tears he paid no mind to as they traced paths down his face.

When he had said enough, he paused, and watched Bailey’s face change. He pushed back his chair with a scrape against the hardwood floor, and moved around the table, coming to crouch just next to his son. After a second’s hesitation, the boy fell into a tight hug, arms coming around to hold his dad as close as Glenn could ever remember. They were silent in each other’s arms, until a pained squeal tore itself from Bailey. They tried to hold on tight enough that they wouldn’t have to let go.

They moved down to the family couch after dinner was forgotten on the table and Glenn explained some more of the details. The bottom line of it all was that he had up to a year left of near-to-full function, before it would likely degrade quickly. The dizzy spells, the migraines, the moments that had convinced him to go to the doctor in the first place, they were expected to get worse over the coming months. Bailey started to cry again, and he held him, this time in tandem with his wife. He placed a gentle kiss on his son’s forehead and whispered softly into his hair.

“I promise you, even when I’m gone, you’re gonna be okay. And until then, I’ll be here for you every single day, whenever you need.”

Bailey fell asleep there in between them. Jess cried silently as she stroked through her son’s hair, staring at the blank TV.

“I’m sorry,” Glenn whispered. She didn’t look over at him. When he held his hand out around Bailey’s sleeping body, she grabbed hold of it. Her tears came faster.

After a while, the crying slowed and then stopped. Glenn picked Bailey up in his arms like he used to do after a long drive, carrying him down the hall and tucking him into his bed. His wife watched from the doorway as Bailey mumbled through the haze of unconsciousness. Glenn thought he could decipher a small “thanks Dad” amongst it. He wasn’t quite sure what it was for.

When they were in their own bedroom, under the warmth of the covers, she switched off the lamp on her bedside table and sniffled, turning away from him as she settled into the bed.

“I don’t know how I’m going to-” A sharp, uneven breath made her pause, and she let it go in a shaky exhale before continuing, “you’re going to leave. How am I supposed to do this alone?”

“I don’t know,” he whispered. “I don’t know, but I know you will.”

Jess fell asleep soon after. He couldn’t do the same, even as the calming sound of his wife’s breaths washed over him as it had for all 19 years of their marriage. Instead, he stared blankly up at the ceiling. He wondered if he’d be able to celebrate when they got to 20 years. He wondered if it mattered if she couldn’t listen to his breathing into their 21st and beyond.


The next day they both notified their bosses that they wouldn’t be in for the day, before Jess called in sick for Bailey. They talked all morning, but largely avoided the sharp ivory tusks and colossal footfalls of the elephant stomping around the room. Even when one or all of them would hold back a sob as tears wrenched themselves out of their eyes, they would stay quiet, just holding hands and comforting in whatever ways they could.

The weeks went by much the same. Glenn took his long service leave to spend more time with the family, but quickly found himself worrying that it was a mistake. Jess and Bailey largely returned to their routines through the week. Mealtimes were quiet, weekends were spent at home. A tiredness dragged at him and he worried his family were feeling the same.

“We should go to the beach,” Glenn said one slow Sunday morning. It was late in the spring, and the weather had been improving over the last month. Jess looked at him with confusion on her face from her chair across the living room. “It’d be nice to get some sun. Might not be too crowded either, since it’s still early.”

“Are you sure that’s a good idea?” Jess asked, putting down her crossword.

“Why not?” He stood and started moving toward his room, pausing for a second as the headrush fuzzed his vision. Once he was steady again, he turned back to his wife saying, “Might as well, I reckon.”

After gathering Bailey from his room, they packed their bathers and towels, put together some picnic supplies and drove down to the beach. They stood where the gravel of the parking lot ended, enjoying the shade of a small tree and looking through its branches out onto the sea. Clear blue water pushed and pulled itself against the shore, softening and darkening the yellow sand and leaving kelp and shells behind as it fell back out toward the horizon. Seagulls trilled and called out as they swarmed above a man eating fish and chips on the pier to the left.

His mouth tugged into a smile as the nostalgic sounds and sights flooded through him. The sea breeze was nice and cool and whipped his shirt around his torso. A longing to freeze the moment in place carved an emptiness into his gut. He didn’t let it shake the joy on his face though, conscious of how Jess watched him as he looked out at the vista. Bailey rushed past him with his towel in one hand and the picnic basket in the other, and he and Jess turned to look at him. They laughed as he hopped from the toes of one foot to the other and back, bouncing across the scorching heat of the sand. Jess leant her head against his shoulder as they watched their son.

A deep sigh moved through her and he placed a hand on her side, rubbing up to her shoulder and back. They walked down to the spot Bailey had picked out, sitting down and applying sunscreen before pulling out some snacks to keep them sustained until lunch time.

They moved to the water not long after, staying close together and being careful not to go too far out. They paddled around, throwing a ball back and forth, splashing and dunking each other. It was light fun, and exactly what Glenn was hoping it would be. As he got a bit more tired, he leant backwards, pulling his feet from the sandbar and letting himself float. The water lapped him around, submerging his ears and putting him in a weightless world of white noise and warm sun. He measured each breath, keeping himself level on the surface of the water. In and out, he felt the world move around him.

After a while of that peaceful existence, Glenn got a little dizzy and decided to return to the shore to get lunch prepared for them. Jess stayed out in the shallows a little longer, while Bailey followed him back to the sand and began working at a castle. Glenn was interrupted in preparing their lunch every few minutes to help mold the spires and assemble the walls. Bailey beamed up at him whenever he managed to empty the bucket just right and leave a perfectly constructed tower in just the right place, before going back to getting the sandwiches ready.

When his eyes opened, his body was sideways on piles of wet, uneven sand, a towel balled up under his ear. Bailey leant over him, knelt in the sand, Jess sitting next to him with her phone tucked between her cheek and shoulder.

He went to sit up, but they both laid a hand on his shoulder and kept him prone. Bread and deli meats were strewn across the sand and towels, and only a single perfectly formed sand structure remained. Bailey was wide-eyed and breathing shakily, the bucket discarded behind him. Water beaded across Jess’ face and shoulders as she tucked her phone away. She and Bailey collapsed around him as he still lay horizontal on the sand. Beach-goers around them outright stared. Glenn tried to choke back tears, stifling them with held breaths and clenched eyelids. A few escaped all the same. He hated himself for it.


The waiting room of the hospital had grown into a fairly known environment for Glenn in the weeks after his beach episode. The beeps emanating from every hallway, the shine of the too-bright lights on the too-polished floors, the strong smell of cleaning solutions, it all became pretty familiar as he waited for yet another scan or test. It wasn’t often that he was in a proper room, however, and the rage of his wife definitely added a new element as well.

Jess barged through the door with fury in her eyes, standing still in the doorway and staring him down in his bed. As ashamed as he was, embarrassed and sorry, her stare felt like a challenge.

He held the eye contact, mouth hardening into a flat line, posture unbothered. And that seemed to make her even angrier.

“Are you out of your fucking mind?”

Glenn fixated briefly on how loud she was in spite of the open door behind her, but he continued to look her in the eye.

She let out a groan and stormed across the room, plonking herself into the chair by his bedside. They didn’t speak for a while. At one point, she stood up again, moving around the bed in a rush. He thought she might just leave, but instead, she went to his other side and swiped his car keys off of the table, shoving them deep into her handbag before going back to her spot in the chair. The motion brought back the guilt in a sharp wave, his idiocy refreshed in his mind, and something inside him softened a bit.

“I’m sorry.” Glenn didn’t shift his focus to her, still just sitting there, bed tilted so his eyes could stare at the wall across from him even as his head rested against the mattress.

“Is that it? That’s the best you can do?”

“What else is there, Jess?” He scrubbed at his eyes.

“You’re a fucking asshole when you want to be, you know that?” She stood again, moving to his bedside so he had to look at her.

“I don’t want to do this right now.”

“Tough.”

“I’m not doing this.”

“Well I am, so get on board.”

“I don’t know what you want me to say.”

“I want you to acknowledge that what you did was reckless and ridiculous and stupid.”

“I fucked up, I get it, of course I get it, what do you want from me?”

“You could have gotten yourself killed!” Her hands were tight around the metal frame of the bed.

“And all I would’ve done was sped up the process!”

She didn’t respond to that. She stepped back and fell again into the chair. It occurred to him that he’d shouted in her face. She was crying when he gathered the regret to look over at her. Her head was in her hands and she was keeping her sobs quiet as they shook her shoulders with their force. His fight withered inside him, and he swung his legs off of the side of his bed, coming to kneel down in front of her. He gently pried the hands off of her face, wiped fingers over her tear tracks and whispered soft, empty words to her.

“I’m tired, Glenn,” she said. “And I just wish that you’d admit that you were too. At least then we could go through this together.”

Of course I am. I’m tired every damned day. But I can’t be.

“I’m sorry,” he said instead. He huddled closer, wrapping his arms around her even as she sat in the chair.

“Aren’t you tired?”
